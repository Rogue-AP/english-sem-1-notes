# Unit 7 Exam Preparation: Listening Skills - Dialects

## Objectives
After studying this unit, you will be able to:
- Understand the meaning of dialect in language
- Become cognizant of dialectal variations in English language
- Find solutions to the problems faced while listening to varied dialects

## Key Concepts

### Varieties of Dialect
A dialect is a form of language spoken in a particular area or by a group, distinguished by grammar, vocabulary, and pronunciation.

**Geographic Dialects:** Regional variations (Yankee English, Australian English).

**Social Dialects:** Variations by social class, education (lower vs. higher classes).

**Slang:** Specific vocabulary, flexible, used by bidialectal speakers.

**Standard Languages:** Dialects used in writing, administration, media.

### Variations within Standard English
- Contracted negatives: I haven’t (south) vs. I’ve not (north/Scotland)
- Indirect objects: She gave the man a book vs. She gave it him
- Participles: I want it washed (south) vs. washing (midlands/north)
- Modals: Must/have to variations
- Have: Stative/dynamic distinctions vary by region
- Like: As filler, intensifier, quotative (be like)
- Verb-particle constructions: Turn out vs. turn the light out

### Traditional Dialects
Conservative dialects in rural areas, differ from Standard English.

**Features:**
- Negation: No/nae instead of not
- Pronouns: Thou/thee (informal singular)
- Gender system: It for mass nouns, he/er for count nouns

### Problems in Listening to Unfamiliar Dialects
- Unfamiliar dialect
- Different spellings
- Variation in pronunciation
- Inability to understand context (social dialect)
- Unfamiliar vocabulary
- Length and speed of speech
- Cultural differences
- Accent
- Quality of recorded materials

### Solutions to Problems
- Attain background and linguistic knowledge
- Seek feedback
- Develop skills: Anticipation, specific information, gist, interpretation, inference
- Remove distractions
- Be patient and empathetic

## Key Terms
- **Dialect:** Regional or social variety of language
- **Accent:** Pronunciation differences
- **Bidialectal:** Able to speak two dialects
- **Slang:** Informal, fashionable vocabulary
- **Standard English:** Formal, written variety

## Self Assessment Questions
1. Slang is used mainly by persons who are ___________. (A. bidialectal)
2. Educational differences between speakers strongly affect the extent of their ___________. (B. vocabulary)
3. In order to solve the problem of listening to unfamiliar dialects, ________ knowledge is needed. (C. linguistic)
4. A dialect is chiefly distinguished from other dialects by features of linguistic structure like ________, ________ and syntax. (C. grammar, morphology)
5. The American English word ‘subway’ is called __________ in British English. (D. underground)
6. The British English word ‘corn’ is called _________ in American English. (A. maize)
7. Yankee English is an example of _________ dialect. (B. geographic)
8. Standard languages arise when a certain dialect begins to be used in ________ form. (C. written)
9. Which of the following is not a problem while listening to unfamiliar dialects? (D. Speaker’s attire)
10. The word ‘dialect’ comes from the ancient ________ language. (B. Greek)
11. We use standard dialects in ________ contexts. (A. formal)
12. A dialect is a form of the language that is spoken in a particular part of the _________ or by a particular group of ________. (A. country, people)
13. Most learners of English learn the ________ dialects of the language. (B. standard)
14. An accent refers to the way in which we _________ words. (C. pronounce)
15. The standard dialect of a language can be spoken with different ___________. (D. accents)
